VLSI Technology: Bipolar Junction Transistor Fabrication

Introduction to VLSI Technology

Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) technology refers to the process of creating integrated circuits (ICs) by combining thousands or millions of transistors into a single chip. This document details the comprehensive fabrication process of Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs) in VLSI technology, covering the physics, chemistry, and engineering principles involved.

Key Technology Note

More than 95% of today's VLSI chips are manufactured using silicon due to its excellent semiconductor properties, stable oxide formation, and abundance. The fabrication process involves precise control at nanometer scales, with each step building upon the previous to create functional electronic devices.

1. Crystal Growth

The foundation of VLSI technology is the single-crystal silicon wafer. Silicon is chosen due to its excellent semiconductor properties, including a suitable bandgap (1.12 eV), high thermal conductivity, and the ability to form a stable oxide (SiO₂).

1

Czochralski (CZ) Method

A seed crystal is dipped into molten silicon and slowly pulled while rotating. The crystal grows as the seed is withdrawn, producing a cylindrical ingot with precise crystalline orientation.

2

Float-Zone (FZ) Method

A polycrystalline rod is locally melted and a single crystal seed is passed through the molten zone to purify and crystallize. This method produces higher purity silicon but is more expensive.

Resistivity Calculation

\[ \rho = \frac{1}{q \mu N} \]
Where: \(\rho\) = resistivity (\(\Omega\cdot\text{cm}\)), \(q\) = electron charge (\(1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}\)), \(\mu\) = carrier mobility (\(\text{cm}^2/\text{V}\cdot\text{s}\)), \(N\) = doping concentration (\(\text{cm}^{-3}\))
\[ N_A = \frac{1}{q \mu_p \rho} \]
For p-type silicon with \(\rho = 10\ \Omega\cdot\text{cm}\) and \(\mu_p \approx 450\ \text{cm}^2/\text{V}\cdot\text{s}\):
\(N_A \approx 1.387 \times 10^{15}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\)

Crystal Orientation: The (111) orientation is commonly used due to its higher oxidation rate and better mechanical stability. The orientation affects etching rates, oxidation kinetics, and electronic properties.

2. Oxidation

Oxidation grows a layer of SiO₂ on the silicon surface, which serves as an insulator, diffusion mask, and protective layer. Silicon dioxide has excellent dielectric properties (\(\varepsilon \approx 3.9\)) and acts as an effective barrier against most dopants.

Oxidation Reactions:

Dry oxidation: \(\text{Si} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{SiO}_2\)
Wet oxidation: \(\text{Si} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{SiO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\)

Wet oxidation is approximately 5-10 times faster than dry oxidation but produces a less dense oxide with lower dielectric strength.

Deal-Grove Model for Oxidation

\[ x_o^2 + A x_o = B (t + \tau) \]
Where: \(x_o\) = oxide thickness, \(t\) = time, \(A\) & \(B\) = temperature-dependent constants, \(\tau\) = initial time offset

Thin Oxide Approximation (\(x_o \ll A\)):

\[ x_o \approx \frac{B}{A} (t + \tau) \]

Thick Oxide Approximation (\(x_o \gg A\)):

\[ x_o^2 \approx B t \]

Example Constants at 1000°C: Dry O₂: \(A = 0.165\ \mu\text{m}\), \(B = 0.0117\ \mu\text{m}^2/\text{hr}\); Wet H₂O: \(A = 0.226\ \mu\text{m}\), \(B = 0.287\ \mu\text{m}^2/\text{hr}\)

Oxidation Color Interference

The oxide thickness determines the color due to thin-film interference: \(2n_{\text{ox}}t = m\lambda\), where \(n_{\text{ox}} \approx 1.46\) (refractive index), \(t\) is thickness, \(m\) is an integer, \(\lambda\) is the wavelength of reflected light. This provides a visual method for thickness estimation.

3. Photolithography

Photolithography transfers geometric patterns from a mask to the photoresist-coated wafer. This is a critical step that defines the microscopic features of the integrated circuit.

Process Steps:

1

Photoresist Coating

Spin-coating to achieve uniform thickness (1-2 μm). Positive photoresist becomes soluble when exposed to UV light, while negative photoresist becomes insoluble.

2

Pre-bake

Removes solvent (90-120°C) to harden the photoresist before exposure.

3

Exposure

UV light (\(\lambda = 365\ \text{nm}\), 248 nm, or 193 nm) passes through a mask, transferring the pattern to the photoresist.

4

Development

Soluble regions are removed (positive resist) or retained (negative resist), revealing the pattern.

Resolution Limit (Rayleigh Criterion)

\[ R = k_1 \frac{\lambda}{\text{NA}} \]
Where: \(R\) = minimum resolvable feature size, \(k_1\) = process factor (~0.4-0.8), \(\lambda\) = wavelength, NA = numerical aperture of the lens

Modern immersion lithography with \(\lambda = 193\ \text{nm}\) and NA = 1.35 can achieve features below 40 nm.

4. Diffusion

Diffusion introduces dopants into silicon by high-temperature annealing (800-1200°C). This process follows Fick's laws of diffusion and is fundamental to creating p-n junctions.

Fick's Laws of Diffusion

First Law (Flux proportional to concentration gradient):

\[ J = -D \frac{\partial C}{\partial x} \]

Second Law (Time-dependent diffusion):

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \]
Where: \(J\) = flux (\(\text{atoms}/\text{cm}^2\cdot\text{s}\)), \(D\) = diffusion coefficient, \(C\) = concentration, \(x\) = distance, \(t\) = time

Diffusion Profiles:

Constant Surface Concentration (Predeposition):

\[ C(x,t) = C_s \text{erfc}\left[ \frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}} \right] \]
Where: \(C_s\) = surface concentration, erfc = complementary error function

Constant Total Dose (Drive-in):

\[ C(x,t) = \frac{Q}{\sqrt{\pi Dt}} \exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{4Dt}\right) \]
Where: \(Q\) = total dose (\(\text{atoms}/\text{cm}^2\))

Junction Depth Calculation

\[ x_j = 2 \sqrt{Dt \ln\left(\frac{Q}{C_B \sqrt{\pi Dt}}\right)} \]
Where: \(x_j\) = junction depth, \(C_B\) = background doping concentration

Temperature Dependence of Diffusion Coefficient

\[ D = D_0 \exp\left(-\frac{E_a}{kT}\right) \]
Where: \(D_0\) = pre-exponential factor, \(E_a\) = activation energy, \(k\) = Boltzmann constant, \(T\) = temperature

Example for Boron: \(D_0 = 0.76\ \text{cm}^2/\text{s}\), \(E_a = 3.46\ \text{eV}\). At 1000°C (1273 K): \(D \approx 1.42\times10^{-14}\ \text{cm}^2/\text{s}\)

5. Epitaxy

Epitaxial growth deposits a single-crystal layer on the substrate, maintaining crystallographic continuity. This is crucial for creating the collector region in BJTs and for advanced device structures.

1

Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)

Silicon source gases (\(\text{SiH}_4\), \(\text{SiH}_2\text{Cl}_2\)) decompose at high temperature (1000-1200°C) to deposit silicon atoms on the substrate. The growth rate is typically 0.1-3 μm/min.

2

In-situ Doping

Dopant gases (\(\text{PH}_3\) for n-type, \(\text{B}_2\text{H}_6\) for p-type) are mixed with the silicon source to create doped epitaxial layers with precise concentration control.

Epitaxial Growth Rate

\[ G = k_s C_s \]
Where: \(G\) = growth rate, \(k_s\) = surface reaction rate constant, \(C_s\) = gas concentration at the surface

The growth rate is often mass-transport limited at high temperatures and surface-reaction limited at lower temperatures.

Buried Layer Out-diffusion

During epitaxial growth, the buried n+ layer dopant diffuses upward into the epitaxial layer. This out-diffusion must be accounted for in device design to ensure proper doping profiles and transistor characteristics.

6. Isolation (PN Junction Isolation)

Adjacent devices are isolated by reverse-biased pn junctions to prevent unwanted electrical interaction. This is achieved through deep p+ diffusion that surrounds each transistor's active region.

1

Isolation Diffusion

A deep p+ diffusion (boron) surrounds each active region, reaching the p-substrate. This creates a p+ region that forms a reverse-biased junction with the n-epitaxial layer.

2

Reverse Bias Condition

The substrate is connected to the most negative supply voltage, ensuring the isolation junctions remain reverse biased during circuit operation.

Reverse Leakage Current

\[ J_{\text{leak}} = q \sqrt{\frac{D_p}{\tau_p}} \frac{n_i^2}{N_D} + q \frac{n_i W}{\tau_g} \]
Where: \(D_p\) = hole diffusion coefficient, \(\tau_p\) = hole lifetime, \(n_i\) = intrinsic carrier concentration, \(N_D\) = n-epi doping, \(W\) = depletion width, \(\tau_g\) = generation lifetime

The first term represents diffusion current, while the second term represents generation-recombination current in the depletion region.

Isolation Challenges

PN junction isolation requires significant chip area and has capacitance that limits high-frequency performance. Modern technologies use trench isolation (STI) for denser packing, but pn junction isolation remains important for bipolar circuits.

7. Base and Emitter Diffusion

The base and emitter regions are formed through sequential diffusion processes. The base is doped p-type (boron) and the emitter is doped n+ (phosphorus or arsenic), with the emitter more heavily doped than the base for high injection efficiency.

Key Principles:

  • Base Diffusion First: The base is diffused before the emitter because the emitter doping concentration is higher and would overwhelm the base profile if done first.
  • Emitter Push Effect: High concentration of emitter dopant enhances the diffusion of the base dopant, causing the base to become deeper under the emitter.
  • Collector Contact: An n+ pocket is diffused simultaneously with the emitter to reduce contact resistance to the lightly doped collector region.

Emitter Push Effect

\[ D_{\text{eff}} = D_0 \exp\left(-\frac{E_a}{kT}\right) \left[1 + \beta \frac{C_{\text{emitter}}}{C_{\text{intrinsic}}}\right] \]
Where: \(D_{\text{eff}}\) = effective diffusion coefficient, \(\beta\) = enhancement factor, \(C_{\text{emitter}}\) = emitter doping concentration, \(C_{\text{intrinsic}}\) = intrinsic carrier concentration

This enhanced diffusion can increase base width under the emitter by 20-30%, affecting transistor gain and frequency response.

Typical Doping Profiles:

Region Dopant Surface Concentration Junction Depth
Emitter Phosphorus/Arsenic \(10^{20}\)-\(10^{21}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\) 0.2-0.5 μm
Base Boron \(10^{17}\)-\(10^{18}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\) 0.5-1.0 μm
Collector Contact Phosphorus \(10^{19}\)-\(10^{20}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\) 0.3-0.6 μm

8. Metallization

Metal layers provide electrical connections to the transistor terminals. Aluminum has been the traditional interconnect material, but copper is now widely used for advanced technologies due to its lower resistivity and better electromigration resistance.

Contact Resistance

\[ \rho_c \propto \exp\left[\frac{2\sqrt{\varepsilon_s m^* \phi_B}}{\hbar \sqrt{N}}\right] \]
Where: \(\rho_c\) = specific contact resistivity, \(\varepsilon_s\) = silicon permittivity, \(m^*\) = effective mass, \(\phi_B\) = barrier height, \(N\) = doping concentration, \(\hbar\) = reduced Planck constant

Higher doping concentration exponentially reduces contact resistance, justifying the use of n+ and p+ regions under contacts.

Aluminum vs. Copper:

Property Aluminum (Al) Copper (Cu)
Resistivity 2.7 μΩ·cm 1.7 μΩ·cm
Electromigration Eₐ 0.5-0.8 eV 1.2 eV
Melting Point 660°C 1085°C
Deposition Method Sputtering (PVD) Electroplating (ECD)

Electromigration: Black's Equation

\[ \text{MTF} = A J^{-n} \exp\left(\frac{E_a}{kT}\right) \]
Where: MTF = median time to failure, \(A\) = material constant, \(J\) = current density, \(n \approx 2\), \(E_a\) = activation energy, \(k\) = Boltzmann constant, \(T\) = temperature

Copper's higher activation energy (1.2 eV vs. 0.6 eV for Al) provides significantly better electromigration resistance, allowing higher current densities.

Aluminum Issues

Aluminum suffers from electromigration (atom movement under high current density) and spiking (penetration into silicon along grain boundaries). These issues are mitigated in copper interconnects through barrier layers and improved microstructure.

9. Ion Implantation

Ion implantation offers precise control over doping dose and depth by accelerating dopant ions to high energies and implanting them into the silicon substrate. This technique has largely replaced diffusion for critical doping steps.

Implantation Profile (Gaussian Approximation)

\[ C(x) = \frac{Q}{\sqrt{2\pi} \Delta R_p} \exp\left[-\frac{(x - R_p)^2}{2(\Delta R_p)^2}\right] \]
Where: \(C(x)\) = concentration at depth \(x\), \(Q\) = dose (\(\text{ions}/\text{cm}^2\)), \(R_p\) = projected range, \(\Delta R_p\) = straggle

The actual profile may deviate from Gaussian, especially at high doses or when channeling occurs along crystal directions.

Advantages Over Diffusion:

  • Precise Dose Control: Can achieve accuracy better than 1% using Faraday cup measurements.
  • Low Temperature Processing: Implantation is done at room temperature, followed by annealing.
  • Profile Control: Multiple implants at different energies can create complex doping profiles.
  • Masking Flexibility: Can implant through thin oxides or other layers.

Projected Range and Straggle

\[ R_p \propto \frac{E^n}{N Z_1 Z_2} \]
Where: \(E\) = ion energy, \(N\) = target atomic density, \(Z_1, Z_2\) = atomic numbers of ion and target, \(n \approx 1.7\) for medium energies

Empirical models (Lindhard-Scharff-Schiøtt theory) or Monte Carlo simulations are used to calculate \(R_p\) and \(\Delta R_p\) for different ion/target combinations.

Typical Implantation Parameters:

Dopant Energy Range Dose Range \(R_p\) (100 keV)
Boron (B⁺) 5-200 keV \(10^{11}\)-\(10^{16}\ \text{cm}^{-2}\) 0.31 μm
Phosphorus (P⁺) 10-400 keV \(10^{11}\)-\(10^{16}\ \text{cm}^{-2}\) 0.12 μm
Arsenic (As⁺) 20-600 keV \(10^{11}\)-\(10^{16}\ \text{cm}^{-2}\) 0.06 μm

10. Complete BJT Fabrication Sequence

The complete process for fabricating an npn bipolar junction transistor involves approximately 20 major steps, each requiring precise control and alignment.

Step Process Purpose Key Parameters
1 Crystal Growth Produce p-type (111) Si wafer Resistivity: 10 Ω·cm, diameter, orientation
2 Wafer Preparation Slice and polish wafer Thickness: 500 μm, surface roughness < 1 nm
3 Oxidation Grow SiO₂ mask Thickness: 0.5-1 μm, dry/wet
4 Photolithography (Active) Define buried layer window Mask alignment, exposure dose
5 Buried Layer Diffusion Form n+ buried layer Dopant: As/Sb, surface conc. ~\(10^{20}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\)
6 Oxide Removal Strip oxide HF etch
7 Epitaxy Grow n-type epi layer Thickness: 1-5 μm, doping ~\(10^{16}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\)
8 Oxidation Grow oxide for isolation Thickness: 0.5 μm
9 Photolithography (Isolation) Define isolation regions Mask design for p+ diffusion
10 Isolation Diffusion Form p+ isolation regions Boron, junction depth > epi thickness
11 Oxidation Grow oxide for base diffusion Thickness: 0.3 μm
12 Photolithography (Base) Define base window Alignment to active region
13 Base Diffusion Form p-type base Boron, surface conc. ~\(10^{18}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\)
14 Photolithography (Emitter) Define emitter and collector contact Alignment to base
15 Emitter Diffusion Form n+ emitter and collector contact Phosphorus/arsenic, surface conc. ~\(10^{21}\ \text{cm}^{-3}\)
16 Oxidation Grow oxide for passivation Thickness: 0.5 μm
17 Photolithography (Contacts) Open contact holes Alignment to emitter, base, collector
18 Metallization Deposit and pattern metal Al or Cu, thickness ~1 μm
19 Annealing Alloy contacts and reduce defects Temperature: 400-450°C, forming gas
Process Integration Note

Each photolithography step requires precise alignment to previous patterns (typically ±0.1 μm). The entire process takes 6-8 weeks from wafer start to finished chips, with hundreds of wafers processed simultaneously in batch operations.

11. Advanced Topics in VLSI Technology

1. Scaling Laws (Moore's Law)

Device dimensions shrink by approximately 0.7× each generation, doubling transistor density every 2 years. The scaling rules (Denard Scaling) maintain electric field constant by reducing voltages along with dimensions.

Scaling Relationships

\[ \text{Dimension} \propto \frac{1}{k},\quad \text{Voltage} \propto \frac{1}{k}, \] \[ \quad \text{Doping} \propto k,\quad \text{Delay} \propto \frac{1}{k} \]
Where k is the scaling factor (>1). This leads to increased speed and reduced power per transistor, but power density increases.

2. CMOS Technology

Modern VLSI uses CMOS (Complementary MOS) for low power digital circuits. BJTs are primarily used in analog/RF circuits where high speed, linearity, or matching is critical. BiCMOS technology integrates both BJTs and MOSFETs on the same chip.

3. Process Simulation

Tools like SUPREM (Stanford University Process Engineering Models) solve diffusion equations numerically to predict doping profiles. Modern TCAD (Technology Computer-Aided Design) tools simulate entire fabrication sequences and device electrical characteristics.

Yield Modeling

\[ Y = \left(1 + \frac{AD}{n}\right)^{-n} \]
Where: \(Y\) = yield, \(A\) = chip area, \(D\) = defect density, \(n\) = clustering parameter. For mature processes, yield can exceed 95% for small chips.

4. Future Trends

  • 3D Integration: Stacking multiple chips or layers for increased density and functionality.
  • New Materials: High-k dielectrics (HfO₂), III-V compounds (GaAs, InP) for high-speed devices.
  • EUV Lithography: Extreme ultraviolet (\(\lambda = 13.5\ \text{nm}\)) for patterning features below 10 nm.
  • Quantum Devices: Exploring quantum effects for beyond-CMOS computing.
Technology Roadmap

The semiconductor industry follows the International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors (ITRS), now replaced by the International Roadmap for Devices and Systems (IRDS). Current research focuses on sub-3 nm nodes, new transistor architectures (Gate-All-Around, Nanosheet), and heterogeneous integration.

VLSI Technology: MOSFET Fabrication - Complete Analysis

Introduction to MOSFET Fabrication

The Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor (MOSFET) is the fundamental building block of modern integrated circuits, particularly in digital logic and memory devices. Unlike Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs), MOSFETs are unipolar devices where current flow is controlled by an electric field rather than minority carrier injection.

Historical Context

Despite the theory of MOSFET operation being predicted by Shockley before BJT theory, practical MOSFETs took significantly longer to commercialize due to difficulties in controlling gate oxide quality and threshold voltage. The first practical MOSFETs were p-channel devices because positive oxide charges made n-MOSFET threshold control problematic.

MOSFET vs BJT Comparison

MOSFET Advantages: Higher packing density, lower power consumption (especially CMOS), simpler fabrication steps, better scalability. BJT Advantages: Higher transconductance, better analog performance, lower 1/f noise.

1. Metal-Gate MOSFET Fabrication Process

The metal-gate process was the first successful MOSFET fabrication technology. Despite its simplicity, it revealed fundamental challenges in device control.

1

Field Oxidation

Start with p-type or n-type substrate (for p-MOS or n-MOS respectively). Grow thick field oxide (8000-10000 Å) using wet oxidation at 900-1000°C. The Deal-Grove model governs oxidation kinetics:

Deal-Grove Model

\[ x_o^2 + A x_o = B (t + \tau) \]
For 8000 Å (0.8 μm) SiO₂ using wet oxidation at 1000°C: t ≈ 4-6 hours
2

Source/Drain Window Patterning

Photolithography to open windows in the field oxide. Alignment tolerance becomes critical as device dimensions shrink.

3

Source/Drain Doping

Diffusion or ion implantation through the windows. Lateral encroachment causes the doped region to extend beyond the mask opening:

Lateral Diffusion

\[ x_j^{\text{lateral}} = 0.8 \times x_j^{\text{vertical}} \]
This necessitates intentional overlap in subsequent gate patterning
4

Gate Oxidation

Grow thin gate oxide (100-200 Å) using dry oxidation at 800-900°C. The gate oxide thickness directly controls threshold voltage:

Threshold Voltage Dependence

\[ \Delta V_T \propto t_{ox} \]
For 100 Å oxide at 900°C: t ≈ 30-60 minutes
5

Contact Metallization

Deposit aluminum (≈1 μm) by sputtering, then pattern contacts for source, drain, and gate. Aluminum's low melting point (660°C) limits subsequent processing.

Critical Issue: Alignment Tolerance

The gate must overlap source/drain regions to ensure continuous channel. For 5 μm technology, typical alignment tolerance is ±1 μm, requiring 2 μm overlap. As dimensions shrink, this becomes a limiting factor.

2. Threshold Voltage Physics and Control

Complete Threshold Voltage Formula

\[ V_T = V_{FB} + 2\phi_F + \frac{\sqrt{4\varepsilon_s q N_A \phi_F}}{C_{ox}} + \frac{Q_{ox}}{C_{ox}} \]
Where: \(V_{FB}\) = flat-band voltage, \(\phi_F\) = Fermi potential, \(\varepsilon_s\) = silicon permittivity, \(q\) = electron charge, \(N_A\) = substrate doping, \(C_{ox}\) = oxide capacitance, \(Q_{ox}\) = oxide charge

Flat-Band Voltage:

\[ V_{FB} = \phi_{ms} = \phi_m - \phi_s \]
\(\phi_m\) = metal work function, \(\phi_s\) = silicon work function

Oxide Capacitance:

\[ C_{ox} = \frac{\varepsilon_{ox}}{t_{ox}} \]
\(\varepsilon_{ox} = 3.9\varepsilon_0 \approx 3.45\times10^{-13} \text{ F/cm}\), \(t_{ox}\) = oxide thickness

Threshold Voltage Components:

Component Typical Value Physical Origin Control Method
Flat-Band Voltage -0.6 to -1.0 V Work function difference Gate material selection
Band Bending (\(2\phi_F\)) 0.6-0.8 V Surface inversion Substrate doping
Depletion Charge 0.5-1.5 V Ionized acceptors/donors Substrate doping, back bias
Oxide Charge (\(Q_{ox}/C_{ox}\)) 0.2-2.0 V Fixed, mobile, interface charges Clean processing, anneals

Fermi Potential Calculation

\[ \phi_F = \frac{kT}{q} \ln\left(\frac{N_A}{n_i}\right) \]
Where: \(k\) = Boltzmann constant (1.38×10⁻²³ J/K), \(T\) = temperature (K), \(n_i\) = intrinsic carrier concentration (1.5×10¹⁰ cm⁻³ at 300K)

For \(N_A = 10^{16} \text{ cm}^{-3}\) at 300K: \(\phi_F \approx 0.35 \text{ V}\)

Oxide Charge Problem

Positive oxide charges (Na⁺, K⁺ ions, fixed oxide charge) shift \(V_T\) negative. For n-MOSFET (enhancement mode, \(V_T > 0\)), this can convert it to depletion mode (\(V_T < 0\)), causing circuit failure. This explains why early MOSFETs were p-channel: negative \(V_T\) becomes more negative with positive \(Q_{ox}\), remaining enhancement mode.

3. Ion Implantation for Threshold Tailoring

Gaussian Implantation Profile

\[ C(x) = \frac{Q}{\sqrt{2\pi} \Delta R_p} \exp\left[-\frac{(x - R_p)^2}{2(\Delta R_p)^2}\right] \]
Where: \(C(x)\) = concentration at depth \(x\), \(Q\) = dose (ions/cm²), \(R_p\) = projected range, \(\Delta R_p\) = straggle

Projected Range (Empirical):

\[ R_p \propto \frac{E^n}{N Z_1 Z_2} \]
\(E\) = ion energy, \(N\) = target atomic density, \(Z_1,Z_2\) = atomic numbers, \(n \approx 1.7\)
1

Threshold Adjustment Implant

For n-MOSFET: implant p-type (boron) in channel region to increase \(V_T\). Required dose:

\[ \Delta V_T = \frac{q \Delta N}{C_{ox}} \Rightarrow Q = \Delta N \times x_j \]
Example: Increase \(V_T\) by 0.5 V with \(t_{ox} = 200 \text{ Å} \Rightarrow Q \approx 5\times10^{12} \text{ ions/cm}^2\)
2

Retrograde Well Formation

High-energy implants create buried layers with peak concentration below surface, improving latch-up immunity and reducing substrate noise coupling.

Implantation Parameters:

Application Ion Energy Dose \(R_p\)
\(V_T\) adjustment (n-MOS) B⁺ 20-100 keV 10¹¹-10¹³ cm⁻² 0.1-0.3 μm
Source/Drain (n-MOS) As⁺ 30-150 keV 10¹⁵-10¹⁶ cm⁻² 0.05-0.15 μm
Source/Drain (p-MOS) BF₂⁺ 50-200 keV 10¹⁵-10¹⁶ cm⁻² 0.05-0.1 μm
Retrograde Well P⁺ 500-1000 keV 10¹²-10¹³ cm⁻² 0.5-1.0 μm
Critical Technology Dependency

While BJTs can be fabricated without ion implantation (using diffusion only), modern MOSFET technology is completely dependent on ion implantation for threshold control, source/drain formation, and well engineering. The precision of implantation (dose control < 1%) enables scaling to nanometer dimensions.

4. Polysilicon-Gate Technology

Polysilicon vs Metal Gate

Polysilicon Advantages: High-temperature stability (mp: 1414°C vs Al: 660°C), enables self-alignment, tunable work function, compatible with silicide formation. Metal Gate Advantages: Lower resistivity, no depletion effects, simpler integration for some applications.

1

Gate Stack Formation

After field oxidation, grow thin gate oxide (10-50 Å in modern technologies). Deposit polysilicon by LPCVD at 600-650°C:

\[ \text{SiH}_4 \rightarrow \text{Si} + 2\text{H}_2 \quad (\text{at } 600-650^\circ\text{C}) \]
Typical thickness: 100-200 nm. Dope polysilicon in-situ or by implantation.
2

Gate Patterning

Photolithography and dry etching (RIE) define gate electrode. Critical dimension control:

\[ \text{CD}_{\text{final}} = \text{CD}_{\text{mask}} \pm \Delta_{\text{litho}} \pm \Delta_{\text{etch}} \]
For 0.1 μm gates: \(\Delta_{\text{total}} < 10 \text{ nm}\) required
3

Source/Drain Formation

With polysilicon gate as mask, implant source/drain. Gate edge acts as natural boundary, creating self-aligned structure.

Polysilicon Work Function Engineering

\[ \phi_{\text{poly}} = \chi + \frac{E_g}{2} \pm \frac{kT}{q} \ln\left(\frac{N}{n_i}\right) \]
Where: \(\chi\) = electron affinity (4.05 eV for Si), \(E_g\) = bandgap (1.12 eV), \(N\) = doping concentration

n⁺ polysilicon: \(\phi \approx 4.05 \text{ eV}\) (similar to Al). p⁺ polysilicon: \(\phi \approx 5.05 \text{ eV}\). Dual work function gates enable optimal \(V_T\) for both n and p MOSFETs in CMOS.

Gate Resistance Calculation:

\[ R_{\text{gate}} = R_{\text{sheet}} \frac{L}{W} \]
Where: \(R_{\text{sheet}}\) = sheet resistance, \(L\) = gate length, \(W\) = gate width
\[ R_{\text{sheet}} = \frac{1}{q \mu N t} \]
\(\mu\) = polysilicon mobility (~30 cm²/V·s), \(N\) = doping, \(t\) = thickness

For doped polysilicon: \(R_{\text{sheet}} \approx 20-30\ \Omega\). For \(L = 0.1\ \mu\text{m}\), \(W = 1\ \mu\text{m}\): \(R_{\text{gate}} \approx 2-3\ \Omega\).

5. Self-Alignment Technique

Overlap Capacitance Comparison

\[ C_{\text{ov}} = \frac{\varepsilon_{\text{ox}} W L_{\text{ov}}}{t_{\text{ox}}} \]
Where: \(L_{\text{ov}}\) = overlap length, \(W\) = gate width

Metal-gate: \(L_{\text{ov}} = \sigma_{\text{lateral}} + \Delta_{\text{align}} \approx 0.07 + 0.25 = 0.32\ \mu\text{m}\) (for 1 μm technology)

Self-aligned: \(L_{\text{ov}} = \sigma_{\text{lateral}} \approx 0.07\ \mu\text{m}\)

Reduction: \(0.32/0.07 \approx 4.6\times\) less overlap capacitance!

1

Gate-First Approach

Define gate before source/drain. The polysilicon gate acts as implant mask. After implantation, only spacers define lightly-doped drain (LDD) regions.

2

Sidewall Spacer Formation

Deposit and anisotropically etch dielectric (SiO₂ or Si₃N₄) to form spacers. These separate heavily-doped source/drain from channel, reducing hot carrier effects.

Self-Alignment Process Sequence:

Step Process Purpose Key Benefit
1 Gate stack formation Define transistor location No alignment to source/drain needed
2 LDD implant Lightly-doped extensions Reduce electric field
3 Spacer formation Dielectric sidewalls Separate LDD from S/D
4 Heavy S/D implant Low-resistance contacts Self-aligned to gate+spacers
5 Rapid thermal anneal Activate dopants Minimize diffusion

Process Window Enhancement

\[ \text{Process Window} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Alignment Tolerance}} \]
Self-alignment eliminates critical alignment between gate and source/drain, increasing process window by 3-5× for the same feature size.

For 0.1 μm technology, alignment tolerance ±0.02 μm would be impossible without self-alignment. With self-alignment: only gate definition needs tight control.

Historical Significance

The invention of self-aligned polysilicon gate technology (c. 1970) was as important as the transistor itself. It enabled scaling from 10 μm to 1 μm technology (1980s), making VLSI and ULSI possible. Today, all CMOS technologies use variations of this approach.

6. MOSFET vs BJT: Detailed Comparison

Process Complexity:

Aspect MOSFET BJT Implications
Mask Count 10-15 masks (modern CMOS) 8-12 masks MOSFET more complex but enables higher density
Thermal Budget Lower (after gate formation) Higher (multiple diffusions) MOSFET better for shallow junctions
Critical Layers Gate, contact Emitter, base, isolation MOSFET has fewer critical alignments
Process Control Oxide thickness, \(V_T\) Doping profiles, \(\beta\) MOSFET requires tighter thickness control

Device Characteristics:

Parameter MOSFET BJT Ratio (MOSFET/BJT)
Input Impedance 10¹²-10¹⁵ Ω 10³-10⁵ Ω 10⁷-10¹⁰×
Transconductance (\(g_m\)) 1-10 mS/μm 40 mS/μm (IC) 0.025-0.25×
Switching Speed Limited by \(R_{on}C_{ox}\) Limited by \(\tau_F\) MOSFET faster at same geometry
Power Dissipation (static) nW-μW (CMOS) mW-MW 10⁻³-10⁻⁶×
Scalability Excellent (to nm) Limited (to μm) MOSFET scales 10-100× better

Current-Voltage Characteristics

MOSFET (Saturation):

\[ I_D = \frac{\mu C_{ox} W}{2L} (V_{GS} - V_T)^2 (1 + \lambda V_{DS}) \]

BJT (Active):

\[ I_C = I_S \exp\left(\frac{V_{BE}}{V_T}\right) \left(1 + \frac{V_{CE}}{V_A}\right) \]
MOSFET: Square-law, voltage-controlled. BJT: Exponential, current-controlled.
Technology Evolution

1970s: p-MOS dominated (simple, tolerant to oxide charges). 1980s: n-MOS took over (faster, better ion implant control). Late 1980s: CMOS became dominant (low power). 1990s: BiCMOS integrated both for mixed-signal. 2000s: CMOS-only for digital, BJT for analog/RF. Today: FinFET/3D transistors extend MOSFET scaling beyond planar limits.

7. Scaling Effects and Limitations

Short-Channel Effects

Threshold Voltage Roll-off:

\[ \Delta V_T = \frac{q N_A W_{dm} x_d}{C_{ox} L} \left[\sqrt{1 + \frac{2x_j}{W_{dm}}} - 1\right] \]

Drain-Induced Barrier Lowering (DIBL):

\[ V_T(V_{DS}) = V_T(0) - \sigma V_{DS} \]
\[ \sigma = \frac{3}{2} \frac{t_{ox}}{L} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + \frac{x_j^2}{L^2}}} \]

Subthreshold Swing:

\[ S = \frac{kT}{q} \ln(10) \left(1 + \frac{C_d}{C_{ox}}\right) \]
Boltzmann limit at 300K: \(S > 60\ \text{mV/decade}\)

Scaling Rules (Denard Scaling):

Parameter Scaling Factor Old Value New Value (k=2) Benefit
Dimension 1/k L = 1 μm L = 0.5 μm Density k² = 4×
Voltage 1/k \(V_{DD}\) = 5 V \(V_{DD}\) = 2.5 V Field constant
Doping k \(N_A = 10^{16} \text{ cm}^{-3}\) \(N_A = 2\times10^{16} \text{ cm}^{-3}\) Maintain depletion width
Delay 1/k τ = 100 ps τ = 50 ps Speed k×
Power/Circuit 1/k² P = 10 mW P = 2.5 mW Power density constant

Quantum Mechanical Effects in Ultrathin Oxides

Direct Tunneling Current:

\[ J_{\text{tunnel}} = A \left(\frac{V_{\text{ox}}}{t_{\text{ox}}}\right)^2 \exp\left(-\frac{B t_{\text{ox}}}{V_{\text{ox}}}\right) \]
Where A, B are constants. For SiO₂ at 1 V: \(J \approx 1\ \text{A/cm}^2\) at \(t_{\text{ox}} \approx 15\ \text{Å}\) (unacceptable for standby power)

Carrier Confinement:

\[ E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8m^* t_{\text{Si}}^2} \]
Energy level splitting for silicon thickness \(t_{\text{Si}}\). For \(t_{\text{Si}} < 5\ \text{nm}\), \(E_1 - E_0 > kT\) (affects carrier distribution)
Fundamental Limits

1. Oxide thickness: Direct tunneling limits SiO₂ to >15 Å (1.5 nm). 2. Channel length: Source-drain tunneling limits to >10 nm. 3. Supply voltage: Subthreshold swing limits to >0.4 V at 300K. 4. Random dopant fluctuation: \(\sigma_{V_T} \propto 1/\sqrt{WL}\) limits minimum area.

8. Advanced MOSFET Technologies

High-k/Metal Gate Technology

Equivalent Oxide Thickness (EOT):

\[ \text{EOT} = \frac{\varepsilon_{\text{SiO}_2}}{\varepsilon_{\text{high-k}}} t_{\text{high-k}} \]
HfO₂: \(\varepsilon \approx 25\) vs SiO₂: \(\varepsilon \approx 3.9\). For same capacitance, HfO₂ can be 6.4× thicker, reducing tunneling current by 10⁶×.

Gate Stack Capacitance:

\[ \frac{1}{C_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{C_{\text{high-k}}} + \frac{1}{C_{\text{IL}}} \]
Interfacial layer (IL, usually SiO₂) capacitance in series with high-k reduces overall capacitance.

FinFET/3D Transistors

1

Multiple Gate Control

Gate wraps around channel (2, 3, or 4 sides), improving electrostatic control. Subthreshold swing approaches ideal value:

\[ S = \frac{kT}{q} \ln(10) \left(1 + \frac{C_d}{n C_{ox}}\right) \]
Where \(n\) = number of gates (2 for FinFET, 4 for GAA).
2

Effective Width Enhancement

Fin height (\(H_{\text{fin}}\)) determines effective width:

\[ W_{\text{eff}} = 2 H_{\text{fin}} + W_{\text{fin}} \approx 2 H_{\text{fin}} \]
For \(H_{\text{fin}} = 50\ \text{nm}\), \(W_{\text{fin}} = 10\ \text{nm}\): \(W_{\text{eff}} = 110\ \text{nm}\) (11× larger than planar).

Strained Silicon Technology

Strain Type Method Mobility Enhancement Application
Tensile (n-MOS) SiN capping layer \(\mu_n +70-100\%\) NMOS transistors
Compressive (p-MOS) SiGe source/drain \(\mu_p +50-80\%\) PMOS transistors
Biaxial (both) Si on relaxed SiGe \(\mu_n +100\%\), \(\mu_p +60\%\) Early strained technology

Mobility Enhancement Physics

\[ \frac{\Delta \mu}{\mu} = \frac{\Delta m^*}{m^*} + \frac{\Delta \tau}{\tau} \]
Strain changes effective mass (\(m^*\)) and scattering time (\(\tau\)). Tensile strain splits conduction band valleys, populating lighter mass valleys.

For electrons in tensile silicon: \(m^*\) decreases by 30-50%, mobility increases 70-100%.

Future Technology Roadmap

Beyond FinFET: Gate-All-Around (GAA) nanowires (2-3 nm), nanosheet transistors, 2D materials (MoS₂, graphene), tunnel FETs (TFETs) for sub-60 mV/decade, spintronics, quantum computing elements. The International Roadmap for Devices and Systems (IRDS) projects CMOS scaling to 1 nm (2028+) with new materials and architectures.
